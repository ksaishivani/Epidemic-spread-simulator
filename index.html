Epidemic Spread Simulator

Introduction About The Project

This project presents an interactive Epidemic Spread Simulator designed to demonstrate how infectious diseases propagate through populations. The system combines grid-based simulation, real-world population behavior, and the mathematical SIR (Susceptible–Infected–Recovered) model to visualize disease transmission and recovery.

By allowing users to select between urban and rural population settings, the project highlights how population density and human contact influence epidemic dynamics. Real-time visual grids and population graphs help users understand both microscopic (individual-level) and macroscopic (population-level) effects of an epidemic.

Population Graph Explanation

The population graph represents the epidemic progression using the SIR (Susceptible–Infected–Recovered) model.

  • Blue Curve (Susceptible): Number of people who are healthy but can get infected.
  • Red Curve (Infected): Number of people currently infected. This rises rapidly in urban areas.
  • Green Curve (Recovered): People who recovered and gained immunity.
  • Orange Dotted Line: Machine Learning–based prediction of future infection trend.

The graph clearly shows how infection spreads, peaks, and eventually declines as recovery increases.

Urban Population Behavior

Urban areas have high population density where individuals live and travel closely together. Due to frequent human interaction, the probability of disease transmission is high. This results in faster infection spread and larger outbreaks in a shorter time period.

Rural Population Behavior

Rural areas have low population density with limited daily contact between individuals. Due to physical distance and fewer interactions, the spread of disease is slower, resulting in smaller and delayed outbreaks compared to urban regions.
